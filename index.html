Defining Homelessness

Homelessness is lacking safe, stable housing.

This includes living in places not meant for habitation, staying in temporary shelters or funded motels, facing imminent loss of housing with no alternatives, or leaving institutions without a permanent place to go. The common thread is not having a reliable home base that meets basic safety and living standards.

Cornell Law School LII

Over 100 million people worldwide have no housing.

According to UN-Habitat, 1.6 billion people live in inadequate housing, with over 100 million entirely unhoused. This crisis spans all nations, driven by factors like poverty, mental illness, domestic violence, urbanization, discrimination, and unemployment, and its severity shifts with changes in any of these conditions.

The Institute of Global Homelessness

2.5–3.5 million Americans are homeless each year.

According to the National Law Center on Homelessness & Poverty, the 2.5–3.5 million figure comes from HUD’s narrow definition of homelessness, which counts only people in shelters, transitional housing, or public spaces not meant for living. This means it excludes millions more—such as those doubled-up with family or friends due to economic hardship—so the actual number of homeless Americans is likely much higher.

National Law Center on Homelessness & Poverty

Causes & Drivers

Poverty and high housing costs drive homelessness.

When incomes can’t keep pace with soaring rent and living costs, maintaining stable housing becomes out of reach. Combined with a severe shortage of affordable homes, this drives many individuals and families into homelessness, even when they are employed or receiving financial assistance.

National Law Center on Homelessness & Poverty

Many homeless people are employed while still homeless.

More than half of people in shelters and about 4 in 10 living outside had jobs in the same year they were counted as homeless. The problem isn’t that they don’t work—it’s that jobs often don’t pay enough to cover rent and basic needs. You can be working hard and still not have a place to live.

Becker Friedman Institute (UChicago)

Most homeless people get some kind of public help.

Benefits like food stamps, Medicaid, or disability checks reach the majority of people without housing—about 9 in 10 in shelters and 8 in 10 living outside. But even with that help, it’s not enough to get them back into stable housing. The safety net helps people survive, but it rarely helps them escape homelessness.

Becker Friedman Institute (UChicago)

Health & Safety

Homelessness violates multiple basic human rights.

It strips people of dignity, safety, and a sense of belonging. Without a home, they lose access to basics like clean water, health care, and personal security, and often face harsh treatment that no one should have to endure.

UN Office of the High Commissioner for Human Rights

Homelessness causes thousands of preventable deaths.

Exposure to extreme weather, poor health care access, and unsafe living conditions lead to thousands of early deaths among homeless individuals each year. These deaths are avoidable, showing a critical failure in state obligations to protect the right to life.

UN Office of the High Commissioner for Human Rights

Homeless individuals have shorter life spans.

Due to constant exposure to unsafe environments, poor nutrition, untreated illnesses, and limited access to consistent healthcare, people experiencing homelessness face significantly reduced life expectancy compared to the general U.S. population.

National Health Care for the Homeless Council

20% of homeless Americans face severe mental illness.

Researchers estimate that over 20% of people experiencing homelessness in the U.S. are living with severe mental illness, such as schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, or major depression. Without consistent treatment and stable housing, they face significantly higher risks of incarceration, suicide, and premature death. Limited access to coordinated mental health care means many cycle between emergency rooms, jails, and the streets, despite their conditions being treatable with proper support.

NAMI Pierce County

Crowded shelters increase exposure to diseases.

Tight living quarters in shelters make it easier for illnesses like tuberculosis, flu, and respiratory infections to spread rapidly among vulnerable populations with limited immunity or medical access.

National Health Care for the Homeless Council

Nutritional deficiencies are common in shelters.

Meals in these settings often rely on cheap, filling foods high in salt, sugar, and starch but lacking essential nutrients, which worsens chronic conditions and weakens immune health.

National Health Care for the Homeless Council

Justice System

Thousands leave jail only to become homeless.

Every year, over 50,000 people go straight from jail or prison into homeless shelters. Many others end up sleeping outside or in unstable situations before finding a safe place to stay. Being homeless right after incarceration makes it harder to find work, stay healthy, and avoid reoffending. This shows how the lack of stable housing after jail fuels the cycle between homelessness and incarceration.

Urban Institute

Unsheltered homeless face more policing.

A California Policy Lab study found that people living without shelter reported an average of 21 police encounters in just six months—ten times more than those in shelters. They were also nine times more likely to have spent at least one night in jail during that time. This shows how living outside greatly increases the risk of criminal justice involvement compared to being in a shelter.

California Policy Lab

Economic Costs & Solutions

One person’s homelessness can cost $40k yearly.

When someone is homeless for a long time and struggles with serious mental health issues, frequent hospital visits, emergency care, and other costly services can add up to about $40,000 each year—often covered by taxpayers. Providing stable housing and support can lower these costs and help people rebuild their lives.

Neighbor.org

Housing investments can cut public costs.

Providing affordable housing alongside healthcare access significantly reduces the use of costly emergency and crisis services. Stable housing creates a healthier environment, improving physical and mental well-being for residents. Over time, these combined benefits strengthen communities, lower public spending, and help prevent and end homelessness.

National Health Care for the Homeless Council

The Housing First program works.

Researchers reviewed multiple long-term studies of the Housing First program across different cities and populations. They consistently found that when people were given immediate, permanent housing without preconditions, the majority remained stably housed for years. The 88% figure reflects the average reduction in homelessness rates measured by tracking participants’ housing status over extended follow-up periods.

National Low Income Housing Coalition

Giving homeless people money can change lives.

A one-time $7,500 payment helped people get out of shelters and into stable housing much faster than usual. Over the course of a year, this led to about $777 in savings per person for the community through reduced shelter stays and lower service use. Recipients used most of the money on rent, groceries, transportation, and other essentials that supported their stability. Very little was spent on alcohol, drugs, or cigarettes, directly challenging one of the most common—and harmful—stereotypes about people experiencing homelessness.

PNAS

Myths vs. Reality

Homelessness is rarely a choice.

The vast majority of people experiencing homelessness say they would move indoors if safe, stable, and affordable housing were offered. Many avoid shelters not out of “service resistance” but because they are unsafe, overcrowded, or fail to meet personal needs. The false belief that people choose homelessness out of laziness ignores systemic barriers and leads to policies that blame individuals instead of addressing root causes like housing shortages and poverty.

Community Solutions

Homeless people are more often victims than threats.

Research shows up to 49 percent of people experiencing homelessness have been victims of violent crime, a rate many times higher than in the general population. They face elevated risks of assault, theft, and harassment simply because they live without secure shelter. Despite this, public perception often wrongly paints them as dangerous, even though studies find they are less likely to commit violent crimes than housed individuals.

Community Solutions

Public myths block real solutions to homelessness.

Many people assume giving cash to the homeless will be wasted on drugs or alcohol. In reality, research shows most spend it on essentials like rent, groceries, and transportation. These false beliefs make it harder to fund programs that could help thousands. Changing the narrative with real data can open the door to proven solutions.

PNAS

Systemic Issues

Homelessness hits marginalized groups hardest.

While homelessness can affect anyone, systemic racism and discrimination mean that certain communities—such as people of color, LGBTQ+ individuals, and those with disabilities—face much higher risks. Barriers in housing, jobs, education, wages, healthcare, and the justice system strip away opportunities and make escaping poverty harder. These inequities are baked into the system, creating a cycle where marginalized groups are more likely to become and remain homeless.

National Alliance to End Homelessness

Most individuals experiencing homelessness are men.

In 2018, about 67% of all homeless people were individuals—meaning they were living alone rather than in a family with children. Among these individuals, men made up roughly 70%, women about 29%, and 1% identified as transgender or non-binary. This shows that single adult men represent the largest portion of the individual homeless population, facing unique challenges that differ from those of homeless families.

National Alliance to End Homelessness

Homeless Americans are left out of poverty statistics.

Despite facing some of the most severe deprivation in the country, people experiencing homelessness are not fully counted in official poverty data or extreme poverty research. They’re also largely excluded from national household surveys, making their struggles less visible in policy discussions. As a result, understanding their economic reality often relies on limited, outdated, or self-reported studies, leaving big gaps in the picture of poverty in America.

Becker Friedman Institute (UChicago)

Sheltered homeless get more help than those outside.

People in shelters usually get more benefits than people sleeping outside. A big reason is that families—who are more likely to be in shelters—often qualify for more programs than single adults. That leaves many living on the streets with less support, even though their needs can be just as urgent, if not greater.

Becker Friedman Institute (UChicago)
